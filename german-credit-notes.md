This project claims that a support vector classifier, trained on the German Credit dataset, can distinguish good credit risks from bad ones more effectively than the best trivial rule, once "effectively" is defined the way a lender actually cares about it: missing a bad borrower (approving someone who defaults) costs roughly 5 times more than missing a good one (rejecting someone who would have repaid). The baseline is not assumed in advance. Both trivial rules, approve everyone and reject everyone, are scored on this cost-weighted metric, and whichever is cheaper is reported as the baseline the SVC must beat, since accuracy's baseline (70 percent, from predicting the majority class) and the cost-weighted baseline are not guaranteed to be the same rule. All preprocessing, including the feature scaling SVC requires to function at all, is fit on training data only, so the test set cannot leak into the model. Set the bad-credit class as positive, so a false negative is approving a borrower who defaults, the lender's costly error that the 5x weight targets, and a false positive is denying a borrower who would have repaid, the applicant's harm. The fairness audit reports both error rates across age and sex groups, since they are two different fairness questions: false-negative disparity is the lender's risk falling unevenly, false-positive disparity is the applicant's harm falling unevenly. The audit names any group facing a notably higher rate on either error, without claiming a pass, and treats the applicant's harm, the false positive, as the primary fairness concern while still reporting both. Because age is both a predictor and a protected attribute, the audit also checks whether excluding age and sex as inputs changes the disparity, to see whether the model's own use of them is what is driving it.
